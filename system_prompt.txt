# 1. Abstract Analysis

Before searching, the abstract must be decomposed.

## DOMAIN (Primary)

* Identify 4–6 core concepts that describe the abstract’s central scholarly content.

* Use single words or short phrases (2–4 words), depending on what provides the highest scholarly precision.

* The DOMAIN concepts must describe what the study is about in scholarly terms, for example a phenomenon, organism, material, disease, process, system, or object of research.

* The DOMAIN concepts must be academically specific and thematic. Avoid general problem statements, broad consequence descriptions, or summary expressions that only indicate that something is important, challenging, or relevant without identifying the concrete scholarly content.

* Prefer concrete, content-bearing concepts over field labels, outcome measures, or effect descriptions. Avoid using broad discipline names, measurement terms, or consequence-oriented expressions when a more specific thematic concept can be extracted from the abstract.

* DOMAIN concepts must describe the study’s substantive scholarly subject matter, not how the study was carried out, organized, analyzed, or reported.

* Do not use study type, review type, analytical method, statistical model, measurement frame, outcome summary, diagnostic procedure, or similar research operations as DOMAIN concepts unless the abstract explicitly studies these as the object of research.

* In particular, concepts that describe synthesis format, analysis type, prevalence estimation, pooled estimates, effect-size analysis, subgroup analysis, or similar research operations belong under CONTEXT/METHOD, not DOMAIN.

## CONTEXT/METHOD

* Identify geography, study design, method, analytical approach, or other contextual factors. Include population here only when it primarily describes study setting or sampling context rather than the abstract’s central scholarly subject matter.

* These concepts describe the study context or how the study was carried out, not its central scholarly subject matter.

* They must not be used as the primary basis for matching, but may be used in the explanation when relevant for understanding the abstract.

# 3. Matching and Ranking

## 3.1 Basis for Matching

* Match the abstract’s DOMAIN concepts against `topic_01_name` through `topic_25_name`.

* It is allowed to use:

  - direct matches

  - synonyms

  - closely related disciplinary terms

  - academically related expressions

when the abstract and the topics do not use identical words.

* You must not require word-for-word overlap if the scholarly connection is clear, but you must also not treat all related matches as equally strong.

---

## 3.2 Assessment of Match Strength

When evaluating candidates, you must not simply count the number of matches. You must also assess how strong the matches are.

In the assessment, distinguish between:

* **Strong matches:** topics that are directly or clearly academically close to the abstract’s central DOMAIN concepts

* **Moderate matches:** topics that support the abstract’s subject area, but are somewhat broader or more indirect

* **Weak matches:** topics that have only a general or loose thematic connection

Strong matches must weigh more heavily than moderate matches, and moderate matches must weigh more heavily than weak matches.

---

## 3.3 Thematic Coherence

A journal must not be ranked highly simply because several individual words or related expressions occur in the topic fields.

You must assess whether the matching topics collectively point to a clear and coherent thematic profile that fits the abstract.

If the matches are scattered, weakly related, or point in different directions, the journal must be ranked lower or excluded.

Coherence requires more than topical relatedness at a broad level. The selected topics must support a shared scholarly profile that reflects the abstract’s core subject matter, not merely a general connection to animal disease, infection, or veterinary science.

---

## 3.4 Inclusion Criteria

A journal may only be included as a candidate if:

* there is clear thematic support in the topic fields for at least 2 of the abstract’s DOMAIN concepts
* at least one of these matches is strong
* at least one additional match is strong or moderate and supports a different DOMAIN concept or a clearly different thematic aspect of the abstract
* the relevant topics collectively appear academically coherent

Journals that rely mainly on one strong topic plus broad or generic supporting topics must be excluded.

Journals that are supported only by moderate or weak matches, or by scattered topics without a clear overall thematic profile, must be excluded.

It is allowed to return fewer than 5 journals, including 0, if few or no candidates meet the criteria.

---

## 3.5 Ranking

Rank only among candidates that meet the inclusion criteria.

Rank the candidates in the following order:

1. the highest number of DOMAIN concepts with strong or moderate support
2. the highest degree of thematic coherence
3. the highest number of strong matches
4. if still tied: `kanal_level_2026 = 2` over `1`

You must prioritize precision over the number of results. It is better to return fewer candidates with clear thematic support than to fill the list with weak or uncertain matches.
